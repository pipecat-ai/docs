## What you'll learn

This guide teaches you how to build multi-agent voice AI systems with Pipecat Subagents. By the end, you'll understand how to decompose complex voice applications into specialized agents that coordinate through a shared message bus.

<Info>
  **Prerequisites**: Familiarity with [Pipecat's core
  concepts](/pipecat/learn/overview) (pipelines, processors, transports) is
  recommended. This guide builds on that foundation.
</Info>

## Why multiple agents?

A single Pipecat pipeline works great for straightforward voice bots. But as your application grows, you might need:

- **Specialized roles**: a greeter that routes to a support agent, a booking agent, or an escalation agent
- **Parallel work**: dispatching research tasks to multiple workers while keeping the conversation going
- **Mixed agent types**: combining an LLM agent with a structured Flows agent for form-like interactions
- **Distributed deployment**: running agents across different machines or scaling them independently

Pipecat Subagents solves these problems by giving each agent its own pipeline while coordinating them through a shared message bus.

## The mental model

Think of it like a call center:

- The **main agent** is the phone system -- it handles the audio connection (transport, STT, TTS) and routes calls to the right person
- **LLM agents** are the specialists -- each has their own knowledge and tools, and they take turns talking to the caller
- **Worker agents** are back-office staff -- they do research or processing tasks behind the scenes and report back
- The **bus** is the internal phone network -- everyone communicates through it without needing to know where others are physically located
- The **runner** is the office manager -- it starts everyone up, keeps track of who's available, and handles shutdown

## Core architecture

Every subagent system has the same basic shape:

<Frame>
  ![Subagents architecture](/images/subagents-basic-architecture.png)
</Frame>

- The **AgentRunner** manages the lifecycle of all agents and owns the bus
- The **main agent** owns the transport and places a `BusBridgeProcessor` where an LLM would normally go
- **Voice agents** are bridged to the bus -- they receive frames from the bridge and send responses back through it
- Only one voice agent is **active** at a time (per bridge) -- the active agent gets the audio frames
